<h2>
	Start Here
</h2>
<p>
	Your personal brand is the frame around everything else you make. Your portfolio, your resume, your site, your emails, and your social profiles all sit inside it.
</p>
<p>
	Your brand is not your logo. It is the whole impression you leave. It is what an employer believes about you after thirty seconds on your site, before they have read a word.
</p>
<p>
	A resume lists what you have done. A portfolio proves it. Your brand decides how all of it is read.
</p>
<h3>
	Your Brand is a Hiring Argument
</h3>
<p>
It is the case you make, in pictures, video, and in words, for why someone should hire you and not the next portfolio in the stack.
</p>
<p>
	Most juniors think the job is to express who they are. The professional move is to position yourself. Your brand answers someone else’s question. The question is <em>why you?</em>
</p>
<p>
	Self-expression feels generous. Self-positioning gets you hired. You need both. Lead with self-positioning.
</p>
<h3>
	What It Should Be
</h3>
<ol class="default">
	<li> <em>Honest.</em> Your brand is a promise. The work has to keep it. If the identity looks more polished than the projects behind it, that gap shows.</li>
	<li> <em>Specific.</em> Stand for something. A clear point of view reads as a real designer with direction. A vague one reads as a blank.</li>
	<li> <em>Coherent.</em> The same name, the same voice, and the same visual identity belong everywhere. Someone should recognize your hand before they read your name.</li>
	<li> <em>Aimed.</em> Point it at the work you want next, not only the work you have done. A brand attracts more of what it already shows.</li>
	<li> <em>Human.</em> Studios hire teammates. Curiosity, personality, and the way you talk about your own work matter more now than at any later stage.</li>
	<li> <em>Defensible.</em> You should be able to state your argument out loud, in a room, in one or two sentences. If you cannot, it is not finished.</li>
</ol>
<h3>
	What It Should Not Be
</h3>
<ol class="default">
	<li><em>Just a logo.</em> A monogram is one small artifact. Perfecting it is not the same as building a brand. The brand is the total impression.</li>
	<li><em>A list of everything you can do.</em> <em>I do everything</em> is read as <em>I do nothing yet</em>. Range does not make you hireable. Specificity does.</li>
	<li><em>A persona the work cannot support.</em> Do not invent a slick agency-of-one act. Overreach gets exposed in the first interview.</li>
	<li><em>Borrowed.</em> Admiring a designer is healthy. Wearing their identity is not. A copied voice is not a voice.</li>
	<li><em>Louder than your skill.</em> Keep the identity in step with the work. A brand that overpromises sets up its own disappointment.</li>
	<li><em>Frozen.</em> You will grow. Your brand should move with you.</li>
</ol>
<h3>
	Specificity, Not Range
</h3>
<p>
	It is tempting to show you can do everything. Resist it. A designer who is a bit of everything gives an employer no seat to slot them into.
</p>
<p>
	<strong>Think of a T</strong>. A wide base of general skill. One deep vertical. An employer should be able to finish the sentence “this is a <em>motion designer</em> who can also handle <em>layout</em> and <em>brand</em>.” Lead with the vertical. Let the rest support it.
</p>
<p>
	Being specific is not the same as being narrow. You are not hiding your range. You are saying what you are first.
</p>
<p>
	Modest and specific beats grand and abstract. <em>A thoughtful typographer who loves print and is learning motion</em> beats <em>visionary multidisciplinary creative</em> every time. The first survives the interview. The second does not.
</p>
<h3>
	The Medium Is Part of the Message
</h3>
<p>
	How you present your work is part of the argument. The container says as much as the contents.
</p>
<p>
	A web designer who builds on a drag-and-drop template quietly contradicts their pitch. A motion designer who fights a fragile hand-coded site burns hours that should go into their reel. Match the vehicle to the claim. Let each piece live in its real medium. Motion belongs on video. A printed piece belongs in your hands, ready to pass across a table. Respect how the work is actually judged.
</p>
<h3>
	4 Questions to Keep Answering
</h3>
<p>
	Come back to these often. They are simple to ask and hard to answer well.
</p>
<ol class="default">
	<li>What do you want to be hired to do?</li>
	<li>What proves you can do it?</li>
	<li>Who is the work for?</li>
	<li>Why you and not someone else?</li>
</ol>
<p>
	Most juniors stumble on the last one. It’s often answered either with arrogance or with apology. Learn to answer it plainly. That is the whole skill.
</p>
<h3>
	It Holds Everything Together
</h3>
<p>
	Your brand is the umbrella. Everything else is a spoke. Your portfolio is the proof. Your resume is the summary. Your site is the stage. Your emails and your socials are the daily contact. Each one should feel like the same person made it.
</p>
<p>
	Build the brand first. Then let it guide every other choice. The colours, the type, the tone, and the platform all follow from the argument you are making.
</p>
<h3>
	It is Never Finished
</h3>
<p>
	A brand is a living thing. It grows as you do. Your point of view will sharpen. Your skills will shift. The work you want will change. Let the brand keep pace. Revisit it. Tighten it. Keep it honest. The goal is not a brand that looks impressive today. The goal is one that still tells the truth a year from now because it has evolved.
</p>
<h3>
	Say It in One Sentence
</h3>
<p>
	Here is the test. Finish this line, out loud, and mean it.
</p>
<p>
	<em>“I am a ______ who ______, and here is the work that proves it.”</em>
</p>
<p>
	Say it plainly. Make sure the work backs it up. Then your brand is doing its job. If the words and the work disagree, that gap is where you start.
</p>
